Your Body's Food Processing Factory

Mouth

Where digestion begins. The mouth uses both mechanical digestion (mastication) and chemical digestion using an enzyme called salivary amylase. The saliva moistens the food and starts breaking down starch into smaller molecules of sugar. The tongue aids in forming the food bolus (blob of chewed up food) for swallowing.

Esophagus

A tube of muscle that connects your mouth to your stomach. Using timed muscle contractions (peristalsis), it pushes the food bolus downward. The upper and lower sphincters in the esophagus help control food entry and prevent the stomach acid from going back up the throat.

Stomach

A big mixing chamber, where protein processing happens. The stomach churns the bolus mechanically and hydrochloric acid and pepsinogen is added into the mix. The stomach is at a pH of 1.5-3.5, which is perfect to activate the enzyme pepsin to digest protein and kill any harmful bacteria.

Pancreas

This is a big enzyme center. The pancreas makes “pancreatic juice” that has sodium bicarbonate, and other enzymes including pancreatic amylase, trypsin, and lipase. It also makes insulin to regulate blood sugar.

Sphincters

The sphincters of the digestive system control the movement of food. The esophageal sphincters regulate entry to the stomach, and the pyloric sphincter controls when the stomach gets emptied.

Gallbladder

The gallbladder stores bile. It concentrates and stores bile made by the liver. When fatty food enters the small intestine, the gallbladder releases that bile, which “emulsifies” fats for easier enzymatic digestion.

Intestines

Small Intestine: Where most food is absorbed. It’s three parts; the duodenum, jejunum, and ileum. It has little villi that increase the surface area 600 fold for maximum absorption of nutrients.

Large Intestine: Water absorption and waste formation. It absorbs all remaining water, electrolytes, and keeps the good bacteria that make vitamins.

Liver

Multiple functions. It makes bile for fat digestion, processes absorbed nutrients, detoxifies harmful stuff, stores glycogen, makes plasma proteins, and processes drugs.

The Enzymes

Pepsin

Location: Stomach
Function: Breaks down proteins into smaller “polypeptides”. Activated from pepsinogen by stomach acid. It works optimally at pH 1.5-2.0, making it perfectly suited for the stomach's acidic environment.

Salivary Amylase

Location: Mouth
Function: Begins digesting starch by breaking down complex carbohydrates into maltose and smaller sugars. It works at a neutral pH but it becomes inactive in the stomach's acidic environment.

Pancreatic Amylase

Location: Small intestine (from pancreas)
Function: It continues breaking down carbohydrates where the salivary amylase left off. It works in the alkaline environment of the small intestine to completely break down starches into maltose.

Trypsin

Location: Small intestine (from pancreas)
Function: It continues protein digestion started by pepsin. It’s activated from trypsinogen by “enterokinase”. Splits proteins at specific amino acid sequences, working optimally at alkaline pH.

Erepsin

Location: Small intestine
Function: Final step in protein digestion! These enzymes break dipeptides and tripeptides into individual amino acids that can be absorbed into the bloodstream.

Why Location Matters

Stomach to Small Intestine Transition

When food moves from the acidic stomach (pH 1.5-3.5) to the alkaline small intestine (pH 8-8.5), stomach enzymes like pepsin become inactivated. The pancreas releases sodium bicarbonate to neutralize the acid, creating the perfect environment for pancreatic enzymes to function.

What If Enzymes Were Switched?

Catastrophic Results! If stomach enzymes moved to the small intestine, they would be inactive due to high pH and couldn't digest proteins. If intestinal enzymes moved to the stomach, the acid would denature them immediately, stopping carbohydrate and remaining protein digestion. This would lead to malnutrition, as proteins and carbohydrates wouldn't be broken down into absorbable units.

Two Types of Digestion

Mechanical Digestion

Physical breakdown of food without changing chemical structure

Locations:

• Mouth: Chewing (mastication) grinds food into smaller pieces

• Stomach: Churning action mixes food with gastric juices, creating chyme

• Small Intestine: Segmentation contracts intestinal muscles to mix chyme with digestive juices

Chemical Digestion

Enzymes break chemical bonds to create smaller, absorbable molecules

Locations:

• Mouth: Salivary amylase begins starch breakdown

• Stomach: Pepsin digests proteins, HCl denatures protein structures

• Small Intestine: Pancreatic enzymes complete digestion of all macronutrients

• Note: Some organs like the stomach and small intestine perform BOTH types!

Breakdown

Carbohydrate Digestion Journey

Complex Carbohydrates → Disaccharides → Monosaccharides

Step 1: Mouth - Salivary amylase breaks starch into maltose

Step 2: Small intestine - Pancreatic amylase continues starch breakdown

Step 3: Brush border enzymes (maltase, sucrase, lactase) break disaccharides into glucose, fructose, and galactose

Absorption: Monosaccharides absorbed in small intestine via active transport and facilitated diffusion, then transported to liver via hepatic portal circulation

Protein Digestion Journey

Proteins → Polypeptides → Dipeptides/Tripeptides → Amino Acids

Step 1: Stomach - Pepsin cleaves proteins into polypeptides

Step 2: Small intestine - Trypsin, chymotrypsin, and elastase break polypeptides into smaller fragments

Step 3: Brush border peptidases (erepsin) break dipeptides and tripeptides into individual amino acids

Absorption: Amino acids absorbed in small intestine via active transport using specific carrier proteins, then enter hepatic portal circulation

Lipid Digestion Journey

Triglycerides → Fatty Acids + Monoglycerides → Chylomicrons

Step 1: Small intestine - Bile salts emulsify large fat droplets into smaller ones (like soap breaking up grease!)

Step 2: Pancreatic lipase breaks triglycerides into fatty acids and monoglycerides

Step 3: Products form micelles with bile salts for absorption

Absorption: Short-chain fatty acids enter hepatic portal circulation; long-chain fatty acids are reassembled into triglycerides, packaged into chylomicrons, and enter lymphatic system before reaching bloodstream

Nutritional Deficiencies

Iron Deficiency Anemia

What it is: Insufficient iron leading to reduced red blood cell production and oxygen-carrying capacity

Impact: Fatigue, weakness, pale skin, cold hands/feet, brittle nails, unusual cravings for ice or starch, rapid heartbeat, and difficulty concentrating

Treatment: Iron-rich foods (red meat, spinach, beans), iron supplements, vitamin C to enhance absorption, and treating underlying causes like bleeding

Vitamin D Deficiency

What it is: Inadequate vitamin D levels affecting calcium absorption and bone health

Impact: Bone pain, muscle weakness, increased fracture risk, delayed healing, depression, frequent infections, and in children, rickets (soft, weak bones)

Treatment: Sunlight exposure, vitamin D supplements, fortified foods (milk, cereals), fatty fish consumption, and calcium-rich diet support

Digestive System Disorders

Peptic Ulcer Disease

What it is: Open sores in the stomach lining or duodenum, often caused by H. pylori bacteria or NSAIDs

Impact: Burning stomach pain (especially when hungry), nausea, vomiting, loss of appetite, weight loss, and potentially life-threatening bleeding if ulcer perforates

Treatment: Antibiotics for H. pylori, acid-reducing medications (proton pump inhibitors, H2 blockers), avoiding NSAIDs, dietary modifications, and stress management

Inflammatory Bowel Disease (Crohn's Disease)

What it is: Chronic inflammatory condition affecting any part of the digestive tract, causing deep tissue inflammation

Impact: Severe diarrhea, abdominal pain, cramping, blood in stool, weight loss, fatigue, malnutrition, and complications like bowel obstruction or fistulas

Treatment: Anti-inflammatory medications, immunosuppressants, biologic therapies, nutritional support, dietary modifications, and sometimes surgery to remove damaged bowel sections
