Even More Harm Than You Think

1. Physical Effects of Smoking (Not Diseases)

Smoking can cause immediate, long term changes to the body that you will see and feel:

  • Reduced lung capacity: The lungs hold less air because the air sacs (alveoli) get damaged and become less "elastic". This means you lose your breath much easier when exerting yourself, or even just walking up stairs.
  • Chronic cough and phlegm production: Your respiratory system tries to remove all the toxic chemicals by making extra mucus, which makes that nasty "smoker's cough" that never goes away.
  • Decreased oxygen transport: Carbon monoxide (CO) from any cigarette replaces oxygen in your red blood cells, so your body doesn't get the oxygen it needs. Thus making you feel tired and weak all the time.

2. Three Major Diseases Caused by Smoking

Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD)

COPD is actually two diseases: chronic bronchitis and emphysema. The toxic chemicals in cigarettes cause chronic inflammation in your bronchi (tubes that carry the air to your lungs), which makes them produce way too much mucus and makes the walls thick, and swollen. Meanwhile, the alveoli get destroyed and can no longer stretch and contract. This makes air gets trapped in your lungs and makes it so you can't breathe properly. People with COPD feel like they're suffocating even when they're just sitting still. My Grandma had COPD before she passed, so I've seen this disease secondhand.

Lung Cancer

Cigarettes have over 70 known carcinogens (cancer chemicals) that directly damage the DNA in your lung cells. When the cells try to repair themselves, they occaisonally make mistakes and start growing uncontrollably, making tumors. These cancer cells can block airways, and also sometimes spread to other parts of your respiratory system and body. Lung cancer usually doesn't show symptoms until it's later stage, which is why it's so deadly.

Pneumonia

Smoking destroys the cilia, which are tiny hairy structures that sweep bacteria and debris out of your lungs. Without them, harmful bacteria can easily get into your alveoli and cause infections. Your immune system also gets weakened by smoking, so it fights infections off worse than usual. The alveoli fill up with pus and fluid instead of air, making it extremely difficult to get oxygen into your bloodstream. This is why smokers get pneumonia more often and have a harder time recovering from it.

3. Effects of Secondhand Smoke

Even if you don't smoke, breathing in someone else's smoke is still harmful to YOUR respiratory system:

  • Immediate irritation: Secondhand smoke contains the same toxic chemicals, making your airways inflamed and produce extra mucus. You might notice coughing, throat irritation, and difficulty breathing right away.
  • Increased infection risk: Just like with smokers, secondhand smoke paralyzes your cilia and weakens your immune system, which makes you more likely to get lung/respitory infections like bronchitis or pneumonia (or both).
  • Long term damage: Regular exposure to secondhand smoke can cause permanent damage to your lungs and raise your risk of developing the same diseases as smokers (because its almost like smoking, just less potent), including lung cancer and COPD, even though you may have never picked up a cigarette yourself.

‚ö†Ô∏è Vaping: Not Harmless, Just Different Harm

Even though vaping could seem safer since there's no smoke, it definitely opens up a whole new can of worms. Ultra fine particles in vape aerosol can get deep into your alveoli and cause inflammation. Some vaping products contain vitamin E acetate, which has been linked to serious lung injuries. Plus, many teens who vape get addicted to nicotine and then switch to regular cigarettes later. The long term effects are still unknown because vaping is so new, but early research suggests it can still cause respiratory problems, just different ones than cigarettes. It's sad to see this even in our school, but as more is researched into the effects, I suspect it will be cracked down on much more.

5. Impact on Our Healthcare System

Smoking puts a massive strain on Alberta's healthcare system in several ways:

  • Increased wait times: Smoking related diseases like COPD, lung cancer, and heart disease require intensive, long term treatment. This means hospital beds, specialists, and medical equipment are tied up treating preventable diseases, making wait times longer for everyone else who needs care.
  • Resource allocation: A huge chunk of our healthcare budget goes toward treating smoking related illnesses. Money that could be spent on improving services, buying new equipment, or hiring more healthcare workers instead gets used up treating diseases that could have been prevented. This affects the quality of care available for all Albertans.
