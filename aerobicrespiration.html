Aerobic Respiration

Cellular Energy Production

What is Aerobic Respiration?

Aerobic respiration is the process by which cells break down glucose in the presence of oxygen to produce ATP (adenosine triphosphate), the energy currency of life. This complex biochemical process is essential for all aerobic organisms, including humans, animals, plants, and many microorganisms.

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP
Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon Dioxide + Water + Energy
Why is this important? Every cell in your body relies on aerobic respiration to produce the energy needed for growth, repair, movement, and all metabolic processes. Without this process, complex life as we know it would not exist.

The Three Stages of Aerobic Respiration

Cytoplasm

1. Glycolysis

What happens: One glucose molecule is broken down into two pyruvate molecules.

Energy yield: 2 ATP molecules (net gain)

Key point: This stage doesn't require oxygen and occurs in the cytoplasm of the cell.

Mitochondrial Matrix

2. Krebs Cycle

What happens: Pyruvate is further broken down, releasing CO₂ and producing electron carriers (NADH and FADH₂).

Energy yield: 2 ATP molecules

Key point: Also called the Citric Acid Cycle, this stage produces most of the electron carriers needed for the final stage.

Inner Mitochondrial Membrane

3. Electron Transport Chain

What happens: Electron carriers from previous stages are used to pump protons and create ATP through chemiosmosis.

Energy yield: Approximately 32-34 ATP molecules

Key point: This is where most ATP is produced and where oxygen is actually used.

Real-World Applications

Why This Matters in Your Life:

  • Exercise: During intense exercise, your muscles may switch to anaerobic respiration when oxygen is limited, causing lactic acid buildup and muscle fatigue.
  • Altitude: At high altitudes, reduced oxygen affects aerobic respiration efficiency, which is why athletes train at altitude.
  • Medical conditions: Conditions like asthma or anemia affect oxygen delivery and cellular respiration.
  • Food industry: Yeast uses anaerobic respiration in bread making and alcohol production.
  • Environmental science: Understanding cellular respiration helps explain ecosystem energy flow and carbon cycling.

Key Terms

ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate): The energy currency of cells

Mitochondria: The "powerhouse" organelles where most aerobic respiration occurs

Pyruvate: The 3-carbon molecule produced from glucose during glycolysis

NADH and FADH₂: Electron carriers that transport energy to the electron transport chain

Chemiosmosis: The process of ATP production using a proton gradient

Substrate-level phosphorylation: Direct ATP production in glycolysis and Krebs cycle

Oxidative phosphorylation: ATP production in the electron transport chain
