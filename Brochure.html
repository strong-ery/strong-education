Lord of the Flies: Free Will vs. Free Won't

A Psychoanalytical Allegory Through Benjamin Libet's Experiment

Allegorical Framework

Benjamin Libet's 1970s experiment uncovered that the brain initiates actions before conscious awareness of the decision. This discovery goes against the traditional idea of free will, suggesting we may only possess "free won't". "Free won't", is a term for the ability to veto impulses after they begin. In the Lord of the Flies, the boys slow but certain descent into savagery shows this unconscious inevitability. Their transformation feels less like conscious choice and more like a compulsive choice stemming from their subconcious. Golding's story highlights how civilization's veneer dissolves when the unconscious overrides rational thought. This perspective shows the novel's deeper psychological truth: we may not choose our impulses, but we have the power to resist them. The island becomes a place where Libet's theories play out dramatically, showing the struggle between conscious intention and unconscious action.

Character Analysis

Jack Merridew

The Savage Leader / The Unconscious Id

Allegorical Connection: Jack represents total unconscious drive. His transformation into savagery happens before conscious decisionmaking. He represents Libet's discovery that actions begin subconsciously. Jack's hunting obsession and very violent impulses emerge from primal brain pathways and patterns, not from rational choice.

Ralph

The Rational Leader / The Conscious Ego

Allegorical Connection: Ralph represents conscious choice and rational decisionmaking. He has great difficulty maintaining civilization through intentional choice, representing humanity's attempt to overcome unconscious impulses through conscious effort and "free won't."

Piggy

The Intellectual / The Superego

Allegorical Connection: Piggy symbolizes total and pure rational thought and the conscious mind's attempt to veto destructive impulses. His glasses (vision/insight) and his constant choice of rationality represent humanity's capacity for the conscious override of unconscious drives or motives.

Symbolic Analysis

The Conch Shell

Meaning: The conch represents conscious democratic choice, and rational communication. The conch needs deliberate action to use; speaking only when holding it represents the use of "free won't".

Allegory Connection: As the boys unconscious motives strengthen, the conch loses its power, showing how conscious control weakens when primal impulses are allowed to dominate.

The Lord of the Flies

Meaning: The severed pig's head represents the unconscious mind's tendency to have dark impulses. The source of actions that begin before conscious awareness.

Allegory Connection: It literally speaks to Simon about the evil within, representing how unconscious motives communicate with consciousness. Which mirrors Libet's brain activity preceding awareness.

Thematic Analysis

Civilization vs. Savagery

Allegory Connection: Civilization represents conscious control and "free won't" (the ability to veto destructive impulses). Savagery represents unconscious motives that initiate before conscious awareness. The boys descent shows unconscious impulses gradually overwhelming their attempts at conscious restraint.

Loss of Innocence

Allegory Connection: Innocence represents the illusion of complete free will. The boys lose this innocence as they discover their actions stem from unconscious drives they cannot fully control, mirroring humanity's realization of the limits of conscious choice.

The Nature of Evil

Allegory Connection: Evil isn't conscious choice but unconscious impulse(s). The novel suggests that destructive actions begin in the brain before moral reasoning can intervene, making evil an unconscious default that requires conscious effort to override.

Critical Quotations

Maybe there is a beast... maybe it's only us.
(Golding 89)
Simon's insight reveals that the "beast" exists within their unconscious minds. The unconcious mind IS the source of the impulses that arise before conscious awareness, perfectly aligning with Libet's discovery of pre-conscious brain activity.
The thing is - fear can't hurt you any more than a dream.
(Golding 82)
Jack's dismissal of conscious fear represents his surrender to unconscious drives. He's abandoning "free won't" (the conscious ability to veto destructive impulses in favor of acting on primal instincts.)
Which is better - to have rules and agree, or to hunt and kill?
(Golding 180)
Piggy's question strengthens the central conflict between conscious choice (rules/agreement) and unconscious drive (hunting/killing). It embodies the tension between free will and predetermined neural impulses.

Benefits of This Analysis

Applying Libet's free will experiment to Lord of the Flies illuminates the novel's psychological depth beyond traditional good vs. evil interpretations. This perspective reveals how Golding almost anticipated modern neuroscience, showing characters as complex beings driven by unconscious processes rather than simple moral choices. It explains why the boys transformation feels inevitable rather than chosen, and why rational characters like Ralph and Piggy struggle against seemingly unstoppable forces. This analysis also demonstrates the novel's relevance to modern debates about moral responsibility, criminal justice, and human nature. If our actions begin unconsciously, how responsible are we for our choices?
